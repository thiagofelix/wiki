### Full Article Content

With last week’s **presidential decree Nº 12.560**, the Brazilian government has officially enshrined the **National Health Data Network (RNDS)** into law. This new legal framework paves the way for nationwide health data sharing—across both Brazil’s **public health system (SUS)** and its **private partners**.

## Health Data Regulation in Brazil

Brazil’s journey toward health data interoperability began in **2011**, when the **Ministry of Health** established mandatory standards for information exchange in both **public (SUS)** and **private sectors**. While these early standards did not include **FHIR**, they mandated **HL7 V2**, **CDA**, **OpenEHR**, and terminologies like **SNOMED** and **LOINC**, with a preference for open and royalty-free solutions.

In **2020**, Brazil launched the **National Health Data Network (RNDS)**, a national platform for integrating and exchanging health information. **States** and **municipalities** can use different or complementary standards, provided they remain interoperable with national requirements. **DATASUS** (the SUS health IT department) was tasked with managing both the **RNDS** and its standards, ensuring compliance with Brazil’s **data protection (LGPD)** and **information access (LAI)** laws.

The latest **presidential decree Nº 12.560 (2025)** strengthens **privacy**, **governance**, and **citizen rights**. It formalizes **digital health platforms** and mandates **transparent, inclusive management** of Brazil’s health data ecosystem. Most importantly, it establishes **official rights for individuals over their health data**—guaranteeing **transparency**, **access**, and **control**. The **Ministry of Health** is now empowered to **define, update, and enforce health IT standards** through **formal governance** and **public consultation**.
