Brazil's telemedicine regulatory framework evolved from a restrictive 2002 baseline through COVID-19 emergency measures to a permanent, multi-layered framework in 2022, now supplemented by AI-specific rules taking effect in 2026. The governing body for physician-specific rules is the **CFM (Conselho Federal de Medicina)**, an autarchic federal body established by Law 3.268/1957. The broader telehealth framework for all health professions is established by federal legislation.

---

## Regulatory Timeline

| Year | Instrument | Key Change |
|---|---|---|
| **2002** | CFM Resolution 1.643/2002 | First telemedicine framework; limited to emergencies or at the attending physician's explicit request |
| **2007** | Ministry of Health telehealth ordinances | First SUS-level telehealth framework; established public telehealth infrastructure |
| **2018** | CFM Resolution 2.227/2018 | Attempted broader regulation covering teleconsulta, telecirurgia, telediagnóstico — later **revoked** amid professional controversy |
| **2020** | Ministry of Health Ordinance 467/2020 | Emergency authorization of telemedicine during COVID-19 pandemic |
| **2020** | Law 13.989/2020 | Emergency legal authorization; expired with end of COVID emergency state |
| **2022 (April 20)** | **CFM Resolution 2.314/2022** | Issued April 20, 2022; published in DOU May 5, 2022. Current permanent physician-specific telemedicine regulation; replaces all prior telemedicine rules |
| **2022 (December)** | **Law 14.510/2022** (Telehealth Act) | Federal law permanently authorizing telehealth across **all** health professions; revokes Law 13.989/2020 |
| **2022** | Ministry of Health Ordinance 1.348/2022 | Telehealth within SUS framework |
| **2024** | Ministry of Health Ordinance 3.691/2024 | "Digital SUS Strategy Action — Telehealth" |
| **2026 (February)** | **CFM Resolution 2.454/2026** | AI in medicine — enters force **August 10, 2026** |

## Pre-COVID Baseline (2002–2019)

The Ministry of Health began issuing telehealth ordinances for SUS infrastructure as early as **2007**, establishing the public telehealth network well before the professional ethics rules caught up.

CFM Resolution 1.643/2002 established a minimal framework that restricted telemedicine to emergency situations or cases where the attending physician explicitly requested a remote consultation. This created a de facto prohibition on routine teleconsultas ([IBA Net](https://www.ibanet.org/telemedicine-regulation-brazil-covid-pandemic)).

CFM Resolution 2.227/2018 attempted to modernize the rules — covering teleconsulta, telecirurgia, and telediagnóstico — but was revoked following backlash from the medical community over concerns about remote-only consultations and market disruption ([IBA Net](https://www.ibanet.org/telemedicine-regulation-brazil-covid-pandemic)).

---

## COVID-19 Emergency (2020)

**Ministry of Health Ordinance 467/2020** and **Law 13.989/2020** authorized teleconsultas exceptionally during the public health emergency. These provisions expired with the official end of the COVID emergency state ([DLA Piper Intelligence](https://www.dlapiperintelligence.com/telehealth/countries/brazil/02-regulation-of-telehealth.html)).

During the pandemic, telemedicine consultations increased by over **200%** ([Research and Markets](https://www.researchandmarkets.com/reports/6209342/brazil-digital-healthcare-telemedicine-market)).

## Permanent Framework (2022)

Two parallel instruments created the current permanent framework.

### CFM Resolution 2.314/2022 — Physician-Specific Rules

Issued **April 20, 2022**; published in Diário Oficial da União **May 5, 2022**. Covers physicians specifically and sets detailed technical platform requirements.

**Synchronous and asynchronous** telemedicine are both expressly permitted.

**First consultations** via telemedicine are allowed — no prior in-person visit is required (except occupational medicine; see CFM 2.323/2022 below).

**Seven recognized telehealth modalities:**
1. Teleconsulta
2. Teleinterconsulta
3. Telediagnóstico
4. Telecirurgia
5. Telemonitoramento / Televigilância
6. Teletriagem
7. Teleconsultoria

([AMIB](https://amib.org.br/resoluc%CC%A7a%CC%83o-no-2-314-de-20-de-abril-de-2022/))

**Key requirements for software and platform providers:**

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Patient informed consent** | Must be captured before each telehealth encounter; includes consent to data transmission |
| **Electronic Health Record (SRES)** | Anamnesis, diagnostic data, and medical conduct must be recorded in the patient's SRES |
| **Security level** | System must support Level of Security Assurance 2 (NGS2) under ICP-Brasil or another legally accepted standard |
| **Digital signatures** | Remote prescriptions and reports require a physician's digital signature meeting ICP-Brasil standards |
| **Remote prescription requirements** | Remote prescriptions must include the patient's address and the location of the telemedicine consultation |
| **Territorial requirement** | Service providers (companies) must be headquartered in Brazil and registered with the state CRM |
| **Physician autonomy** | Physician decides whether to use telemedicine; cannot be contractually removed |
| **Physician liability** | Attending physician is individually liable; the platform/employer is jointly liable in proportion to harm caused |
| **Recording** | Recording of telemedicine sessions is NOT mandatory |
| **Platform technology** | General videoconferencing tools (Zoom, Teams, Google Meet) are permitted; the compliance concern is data privacy, not platform type |
| **Data storage and security** | Must follow CFM rules on storage, integrity, confidentiality, privacy, and professional secrecy |
| **Chronic disease follow-up** | Patients with chronic conditions must have an in-person visit at least every **180 days** |
| **Supervision** | Compliance enforced by the respective state CRM |

([CMS Law](https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-digital-health-apps-and-telemedicine/brazil), [AMB](https://amb.org.br/cfm-regulamenta-consultas-de-telemedicina-no-brasil/))

### CFM Resolution 2.323/2022 — Occupational Medicine Exception

**CFM Resolution 2.323/2022** explicitly **prohibits occupational medicine exams via telemedicine**. This is the primary carve-out from the general rule allowing first consultations remotely. In-person attendance remains mandatory for occupational health assessments.

### Law 14.510/2022 — Telehealth Act (All Professions)

Signed December 28, 2022; entered into force immediately.

- Permanently authorizes telehealth across **all** health professions governed by Professional Councils
- Defines telehealth broadly: remote service delivery using ICT, including texts, sounds, and images
- Delegates ethics regulation to each profession's council (CFM for physicians, COFEN for nurses, etc.)
- Revokes Law 13.989/2020 (the COVID emergency authorization)
- Affirms [[lgpd-health-data]] compliance obligations for all telehealth services
- SUS telehealth services are **free for patients**
- Intermediary companies providing telehealth platforms must register with their **state CRM**

**Nine explicit principles** enshrined in the law:
1. Patient's right to refuse telehealth — a guaranteed in-person alternative must exist
2. Dignity of health professionals
3. Universal access to telehealth services
4. Digital responsibility
5. Strict scope of practice (each professional acts only within their licensed competency)
6. LGPD / [[lgpd-health-data]] compliance
7. Compliance with the **Marco Civil da Internet** (Internet Civil Rights Framework)
8. Compliance with the **Consumer Protection Code (CDC)**
9. Non-regression clause: any future restriction on telehealth requires demonstrating that the restriction is "indispensable to avoid patient harm"

**Cross-state practice:** Telehealth acts are valid throughout Brazil — secondary CRM registration in the patient's state is **waived** for cross-state teleconsultas.

**Consumer liability note:** B2C telehealth platforms face **strict liability** under the CDC for software defects. Under the Marco Civil da Internet, fines can reach up to **10% of the Brazilian group's annual revenue**.

([Souto Correa](https://www.soutocorrea.com.br/en/client-alerts/lei-que-regulamenta-a-telessaude-no-brasil-e-sancionada-e-entra-em-vigor-imediatamente/), [Demarest](https://www.demarest.com.br/en/law-that-regulates-telehealth-sector-sanctioned-by-the-president-of-brazil/), [IBA Net](https://www.ibanet.org/telemedicine-regulation-brazil-covid-pandemic), [DLA Piper Intelligence](https://www.dlapiperintelligence.com/telehealth/countries/brazil/02-regulation-of-telehealth.html))

## Other Profession-Specific Telehealth Rules

Each health-profession council issues its own telehealth ethics regulation under the framework established by Law 14.510/2022. Key resolutions:

| Profession | Instrument | Year |
|---|---|---|
| **Nursing** | COFEN Resolution 696/2022 | 2022 |
| **Psychology** | CFP Resolution 9/2024 | 2024 |
| **Nutrition** | CFN Resolution 760/2023 | 2023 |
| **Pharmacy** | CFF Resolution 727/2022 | 2022 |
| **Physical therapy / Occupational therapy** | COFFITO Resolution 516/2020 | 2020 |

Software platforms serving multiple health professions must verify compliance with each applicable council's resolution, not only CFM 2.314/2022.

## Health Plan Coverage — ANS Technical Note 6/2020

**ANS Technical Note 6/2020** establishes that health plans regulated by ANS must cover **teleconsultas** for any procedure already listed in the Rol de Procedimentos e Eventos em Saúde (the mandatory coverage list). This applies to both synchronous and asynchronous modalities where included.

## AI in Medicine — CFM Resolution 2.454/2026

**Published: February 27, 2026. Effective date: August 10, 2026** (180-day compliance window from publication).

This resolution is the first Brazilian regulation specifically governing AI use in clinical practice.

### Key Provisions

| Provision | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Permitted use** | AI tools expressly permitted as **support instruments** for clinical decision-making, health management, research, and continuing education |
| **Mandatory human supervision** | AI cannot replace the physician's final authority over clinical decisions |
| **Prohibited: AI-to-patient communication** | AI systems are **prohibited** from communicating diagnoses or prognoses directly to patients — a physician must be the communicating party |
| **Continuous auditing** | Institutions must implement continuous auditing and monitoring of AI tools; **prompt/response audit logs** required |
| **RAG architecture** | Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architecture is specifically recommended for clinical AI implementations |
| **AI and Telemedicine Committee** | A dedicated **Comissão de IA e Telemedicina** under medical coordination is required |
| **Risk classification** | AI tools must be classified as **Low / Medium / High / Unacceptable** risk (four tiers) |
| **Unacceptable-risk category** | Certain AI use cases are classified as categorically unacceptable |
| **Physician liability** | Physician liability is **excluded** for AI-exclusive failures if the physician demonstrates diligent use |
| **Physician right of refusal** | Institutions **cannot penalize** physicians who decline to follow AI recommendations |
| **Shadow AI** | Unauthorized AI use ("Shadow AI") is classified as a **catastrophic institutional risk** |
| **Transparent reporting** | Reporting requirements for AI use outcomes |
| **Documentation** | AI use in clinical decision-making must be recorded in the patient's medical record |

([JD Supra](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/brazilian-cfm-issues-resolution-on-the-6731720/), [Machado Meyer](https://www.machadomeyer.com.br/pt/inteligencia-juridica/publicacoes-ij/life-sciences-e-saude/cfm-regulamenta-o-uso-de-inteligencia-artificial-na-medicina), [Medicina S/A](https://medicinasa.com.br/eval-ia-corporativa/))

The resolution creates **four risk tiers** (Low / Medium / High / Unacceptable) that map implicitly to [[anvisa-samd]] SaMD classification requirements. The same risk classification framework is used in **PL 2.338/2023**, the pending Brazilian AI Law (pending before Congress). Software vendors offering AI-assisted diagnostics or clinical decision support will need to implement governance frameworks before August 10, 2026.

## Market Impact

- **68% of Brazilian health institutions** offer telemedicine services in 2025 (Panorama Clínicas e Hospitais 2025)
- [[doctoralia]]: 3 million online consultations in 2024, up 57% versus 2023 ([Doctoralia](https://pro.doctoralia.com.br/blog/clinicas/telemedicina-o-que-diz-a-lei-brasileira))
- Over **30 million telemedicine consultations** recorded in Brazil in 2024 ([LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ash-frenken-86298042_brasil-vai-de-pa%C3%ADs-das-fintechs-ao-pa%C3%ADs-das-activity-7435870168297316352-44-A))

## Summary: Software Compliance Checklist for Telemedicine Platforms

| Regulation | Requirement |
|---|---|
| CFM 2.314/2022 | SRES integration; ICP-Brasil NGS2 digital signatures; consent capture; physician autonomy controls; state CRM registration; patient address + consultation location in remote prescriptions; recording NOT mandatory |
| CFM 2.323/2022 | Occupational medicine exams must be conducted in-person — telehealth prohibited |
| Law 14.510/2022 | LGPD compliance; Marco Civil da Internet compliance; CDC compliance; cross-state CRM registration waived; in-person alternative must be available to any patient who refuses telehealth |
| ANS Technical Note 6/2020 | Health plans must cover teleconsultas for Rol-listed procedures |
| CFM 2.454/2026 (AI) | Four-tier AI risk classification; Comissão de IA e Telemedicina under medical coordination; prompt/response audit logs; physician override capability; prohibit AI-to-patient diagnosis communication; no penalties for refusing AI recommendations |
| [[lgpd-health-data]] | Explicit consent; DPO; breach notification; encryption |
| [[anvisa-samd]] | If software makes clinical decisions → ANVISA SaMD registration required |
| Marco Civil da Internet | Fine up to 10% of Brazilian group revenue for violations |
| Consumer Protection Code (CDC) | Strict liability for software defects in B2C platforms |
